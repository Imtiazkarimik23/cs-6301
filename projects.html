Introduction

In this semester-long project, students will conduct research on network or systems security in teams of 1–2 people under the instructor's supervision. All teams and project topics must be approved by the instructor. The outcome of this project will be similar to a conference-style paper of at most 10 pages, in which you build an argument for studying the open research problems you advocate investigating—identifying why the problem is important and why it is solvable in the near future. The grade will be based on novelty, depth, correctness, rigor of execution, clarity of presentation, and effort.

Project Theme

“Thinking like an adversary” is critical to understanding (1) the current security and privacy posture of a system, (2) how existing security policies can be broken, and (3) how to enforce stronger and more robust security policies. Accordingly, each project should align with the theme of security analysis of an existing system.

The overarching goal is to design and implement a systematic security evaluation framework using program analysis, software testing, formal verification, and applied cryptography. Each project consists of two parts, each with multiple milestones.

Part 1 – Reproducing an Existing Attack

As the first step, you will reproduce a known attack on an existing system. Your task is to understand a known vulnerability and its corresponding attack and write an original exploit. You may not contact the paper authors for exploits or use pre-packaged attack tools. However, you may use open-source, general-purpose tools (e.g., gdb, Wireshark, packet sniffers) and scripts.

Some vulnerabilities may have been fixed in newer software or libraries (e.g., Linux, TCP, TLS), so you may need to target older versions.

According to the course ethics policy, you must not test attacks against systems owned by others. All attacks must be conducted against your own isolated device, machine, or virtual machine.

You will present a demo showing (1) the vulnerability, (2) how it can be exploited, and (3) the implications of the attack. You will also explain how you implemented the exploit, set up the attack environment, and the technical challenges encountered.

Part 2 – Project Extensions

Based on insights gained from Part 1, you may extend your project in one or more of the following directions:

  • Identify a new attack on the same system or protocol.
  • Design a systematic security analysis framework using formal verification, static or dynamic analysis, fuzzing, or other testing techniques.
  • Propose verified or high-assurance defenses.

Project Proposal

The proposal must not exceed two pages (including references). Use the ACM Overleaf template for all project-related submissions.

Proposal Components

  • What (Problem Statement): Project title, team member contact details, problem statement, goals, and the specific attack(s) to be reproduced.
  • Why (Motivation): Importance of the problem, relevant prior work, and why the vulnerability is critical and difficult to detect manually.
  • How (Design / Implementation): Resources required, plans for Part 2, related codebases, and evaluation criteria (e.g., effectiveness, efficiency).
  • Workload: Task distribution among team members and milestones for Part 1.

Final Report

You must submit a minimum 8-page (excluding references) conference- or workshop-style paper. Typical sections include Abstract, Introduction, Background, Design Overview, Design Details, Implementation, Evaluation, Discussion and Limitations, Related Work, and Conclusion and Future Work.

Guidelines for Key Sections

Abstract

The abstract should be approximately 300 words and include: area, problem, solution idea, methodology, results, and take-away.

Introduction

Motivate the research problem, define the problem statement, justify why existing solutions are insufficient, present your proposal and intuitions, and summarize high-level results.

Background

Provide only the background necessary to understand the paper. Avoid unrelated or unused details.

Design Overview

Describe adversary assumptions, design challenges, high-level intuitions, and a system overview. Diagrams are encouraged.

Design Details

Discuss all system components, critical corner cases, and design rationale at a conceptual level.

Implementation

Describe the software and hardware used and how modules interact to realize the system design.

Evaluation

Describe the evaluation setup, metrics, datasets, baselines, and experimental results using tables and figures.

Related Work

Present a narrative comparison of prior work, highlighting differences and advances relative to your approach.
